§01

Brief

THE COMPETITION

The ASME Innovative Additive Manufacturing 3D (IAM3D) Competition: Remote Operations Vehicle for the Excavation of Resources (R.O.V.E.R.) tasks university students to design an unmanned vehicle capable of gathering and depositing resources on an extraterrestrial landscape. The vehicle must fit within a 30.48 cm cube, be primarily 3D printed, and navigate a 2.43 m × 2.43 m sand course to excavate and deposit randomly placed resources into a central container — all controlled via FPV from an enclosed command center with no direct line of sight.

THE CHALLENGE

The vehicle needed to traverse loose sand, pick up resources ranging from 1 cm to 10.16 cm in size, and deposit them reliably — all within strict size constraints and with a heavy scoring emphasis on additive manufacturing ratio. The sand terrain demanded high traction, the excavation required a robust scoop mechanism that could both lift and tilt, and the entire system had to be remotely operated with zero direct visibility.

OUR APPROACH

We designed the entire rover from scratch over 4 design iterations, achieving a 1:1 additive manufacturing ratio. The chassis and drivetrain evolved from a wheeled configuration to a custom track system for superior sand traction. A lead-screw-driven scoop with a 4-bar linkage provides reliable excavation, while an ESP32-based control system with WiFi FPV keeps the electronics minimal and effective.

§03

Iterative Design

The rover went through 4 major design iterations, each addressing failures and limitations discovered during testing. The first iteration used a wheeled drivetrain, but testing in loose sand revealed insufficient traction — the wheels would dig in and stall. We pivoted to a tracked drivetrain for all subsequent iterations.

The first track design used 4 individual links joined together with screws and plates. This proved overly complex to assemble and prone to binding. We redesigned the track as a single continuous link per side, significantly simplifying assembly and improving reliability.

The final design features a track contact length of 20 cm, track width of 7.6 cm, and ground clearance of 6 cm — optimized for stability and traction on the sand course.

§04

Mechanical

DRIVETRAIN

4WD tracked configuration using four Henkwell 24V DC motors (142 RPM no-load, 5.6 kgf·cm stall torque, 1.5 kgf·cm rated torque). The tracked system provides significantly better traction over loose sand compared to wheels, distributing the vehicle's 2.2 kg weight over a larger contact area.

SCOOP MECHANISM

The scoop is lifted using a NEMA 17 stepper motor coupled with a T8 threaded rod and lead screw, providing precise vertical positioning and enough holding force to keep the scoop raised under load. For tilting the scoop to dump resources, two MG996R servos drive a 4-bar linkage that multiplies torque for reliable operation.

ADDITIVE MANUFACTURING

The competition scoring is heavily weighted on the percentage of parts produced using additive manufacturing — the AM ratio directly determines a significant portion of the total score. Our rover achieved a 1:1 additive manufacturing ratio, meaning every non-exempt part (electronics, motors, fasteners, batteries, and wiring are exempt per the rules) was 3D printed by the team.

The chassis, drive wheels, and track components were printed in PLA for its rigidity and dimensional accuracy, while the scoop and stepper/lead-screw mounts were printed in PETG for its superior impact resistance and slight flexibility under load. All parts are original designs — the use of pre-existing designs is grounds for disqualification.

§05

Electronics

CONTROL SYSTEM

An ESP32 microcontroller serves as the brain of the rover, communicating over WiFi with a custom control center GUI running on a phone. The drive motors are managed by 2× L298N dual H-bridge motor drivers (one per side for skid-steer control), and the NEMA 17 stepper is driven by an A4988 stepper motor driver. The entire system is powered by a 4S LiPo battery, providing the 14.8V nominal voltage needed to drive the 24V motors at a usable speed while keeping the package compact and lightweight.

FPV SYSTEM

Since the competition requires fully remote operation from an enclosed command center with no direct line of sight, we mounted a phone inside the scoop area running IPCam over WiFi. The video feed streams to a laptop in the command booth, giving the operator a first-person view for navigating the course and scooping resources.

§06

Analysis & Report

FEA SIMULATION

Finite Element Analysis was performed on critical structural components in SolidWorks Simulation as part of the pre-competition design report. Stress analysis on the chassis frame, scoop, and lead-screw mounts validated that 3D printed parts could handle the expected loading conditions without failure, helping justify material choices (PLA vs PETG) and wall thicknesses before committing to print runs.

DESIGN REPORT

A comprehensive design report was submitted as a pre-competition deliverable, including individual 2D CAD drawings for every part, full assembly drawings with bill of materials, exploded views, structural FEA results, Design for Manufacture and Assembly (DFMA) analysis, Design for Additive Manufacturing (DFAM) analysis, a complete record of all 4 design iterations with rationale, and testing documentation.
